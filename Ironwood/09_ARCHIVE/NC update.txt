


North Carolina Digital Infrastructure & AI
Comprehensive Analysis of the North Carolina Digital Economy: Infrastructure, AI Integration, and Regulatory Frameworks
1. Introduction and Macroeconomic Context
The global transition toward an intelligence-driven, computationally intensive economy has catalyzed the most expansive peacetime infrastructure build-out in modern history. At the epicenter of this transformation within the United States is North Carolina. Historically reliant on traditional manufacturing, agriculture, and textiles, the state has systematically engineered a metamorphosis into a premier destination for digital infrastructure, artificial intelligence (AI) research, and advanced telecommunications. Information processing investment contributed more than half of the 1.2% growth in U.S. GDP during the first half of 2025 alone, with total global investments in upcoming and under-construction data centers soaring to an estimated $750 billion.   

North Carolina captures a disproportionate share of this capital influx due to a confluence of strategic advantages: robust electrical grid coverage provided by Duke Energy, an abundance of developable land, aggressive statutory tax incentives, and highly concentrated intellectual capital emanating from the Research Triangle. Consequently, the state is experiencing a secondary wave of high-density infrastructure deployment specifically optimized for AI, machine learning, and advanced cloud computing architectures. AI-related venture capital is reaching all corners of the state, with local tech employment growing two-and-a-half times faster than the broader economy.   

However, this hyper-growth introduces profound systemic frictions. The integration of gigawatt-scale data facilities places unprecedented strain on the state’s energy grid, precipitating debates over utility ratepayer equity, environmental sustainability, and the necessity of targeted regulatory interventions. Furthermore, as advanced algorithmic systems permeate the public and private sectors, state lawmakers are grappling with the complexities of AI governance, navigating the tension between fostering innovation and safeguarding consumer rights amidst sweeping federal preemption mandates.   

This report provides an exhaustive, peer-level analysis of North Carolina's digital infrastructure and market landscape as of early 2026. It dissects the deployment of foundational broadband networks, the explosive growth of the data center corridor, the burgeoning AI startup ecosystem, and the evolving legal paradigms governing these critical technologies.

2. Foundational Connectivity: Broadband and Fiber-Optic Networks
Fiber-optic infrastructure constitutes the physical substrate of the digital economy. It is the mandatory prerequisite for both equitable residential access and the ultra-low-latency, high-capacity architectures required by hyperscale data centers. Data centers increasingly rely on advanced fiber designs, including multi-core and hollow-core fiber, to support extreme capacity and dense interconnection, while single-mode fiber remains the bedrock for access networks connecting homes and businesses.   

2.1 The Scope of the Digital Divide and Federal Intervention
Despite its status as a technology hub, North Carolina possesses the second-largest rural population in the United States, presenting severe topographical and economic hurdles to ubiquitous broadband deployment. The state currently ranks 38th nationally in comprehensive broadband coverage, speed, and availability. While 85.8% of the population has access to baseline broadband (defined by the FCC as 25 Mbps download and 3 Mbps upload), fiber-optic availability remains constrained to 43.5% of residents. Furthermore, North Carolina ranks 45th among all states for gigabit broadband access, with only 42.8% of the population having access to 1 Gbps speeds.   

In December 2023, data from internet service providers (ISPs) indicated that approximately 344,000 homes and businesses—representing 8% of the state's 4.3 million broadband serviceable locations—lacked sufficient internet speeds. To rectify this market failure, the North Carolina Department of Information Technology (NCDIT) Division of Broadband and Digital Opportunity initiated a multi-layered funding apparatus. The GREAT grant and Completing Access to Broadband (CAB) programs, fueled by the American Rescue Plan Act (ARPA), are currently constructing service to 255,570 locations.   

The remaining 2% of unserved locations are targeted by the federal Broadband Equity, Access, and Deployment (BEAD) program, authorized by the Bipartisan Infrastructure Law, which allocated over $1.5 billion to the state. The technological distribution of these BEAD deployments reflects a highly pragmatic approach to the state's challenging geography, utilizing alternative delivery mechanisms where traditional fiber trenching is economically unviable.   

Delivery Technology	Percentage of BEAD Locations	Strategic Rationale and Deployment Context

The regulatory environment surrounding these deployments shifted dynamically in mid-2025. On June 6, 2025, the National Telecommunications and Information Administration (NTIA) issued a Restructuring BEAD Policy Notice. This directive mandated that unlicensed fixed wireless providers submit rigorous technical documentation proving their networks could reliably deliver subgrant-standard service for at least four years. If proven, those locations were removed from BEAD eligibility, narrowing the state's final eligible location count to 171,573.   

Concurrently, the FCC enforces stringent oversight via the Rural Broadband Accountability Plan (RBAP). This program explicitly monitors compliance for high-cost Universal Service Fund (USF) and Rural Digital Opportunity Fund (RDOF) programs. Under the RBAP, the FCC doubled the number of audits and verifications, instituting mandatory on-site audits and stringent speed and latency performance testing to ensure capital efficiency.   

2.2 Regional Infrastructure Analysis: Craven County
A granular examination of Craven County illustrates the localized impacts of these macro-level policies. As a mid-sized coastal community, Craven County demonstrates an overall Broadband Availability Index wherein 98.39% of the population has access to 25/3 Mbps service, and 95.81% can access 100/20 Mbps speeds. However, fiber availability lags significantly at 48.2%.   

To bridge this gap, Spectrum (Charter Communications) launched a massive expansion of its fiber-optic network, subsidized by a $1 billion national allocation from the FCC's RDOF auction. This buildout delivers gigabit broadband to over 200 historically unserved homes and small businesses in the county. In tandem, the state CAB program allocated further funding to connect an additional 2,731 locations within Craven County, effectively saturating the region through coordinated public-private capital. This improved digital bedrock directly supports advanced industrial activity, evidenced by Chatsworth Products, Inc. (CPI) investing $11 million to expand its New Bern IT equipment manufacturing facility, adding 45 advanced manufacturing jobs.

3. The Data Center Topography and Supply Chain Dynamics
The rise of artificial intelligence workloads is fundamentally rewiring the data center industry. AI dictates unprecedented power density, driving occupiers to prioritize markets with scalable baseload power capacity and advanced connectivity. As traditional primary hubs like Northern Virginia face acute congestion and water scarcity, secondary markets are absorbing the overflow. North Carolina has actively cultivated a "data center corridor" that is transforming its physical and economic landscape.   

3.1 Capacity, Clustering, and Mega-Projects
At the close of 2024, supply in primary North American data center markets increased by 34% year-over-year to 6,922.6 megawatts (MW), with primary markets hosting a record 6,350 MW under construction. Vacancy rates plummeted to a record low of 1.9%, reflecting the intense scarcity of available large-scale inventory. North Carolina currently hosts nearly 100 operating facilities spread across 18 distinct markets, with total active and planned capacity expected to roughly double over the next decade.   

The geographic distribution of these facilities reveals a bimodal strategy: dense colocation clustering in urban centers, and sprawling hyperscale campuses in rural counties.

The current "AI era" is characterized by infrastructure projects of staggering scale. Most notably, Amazon Web Services (AWS) announced a $10 billion investment to construct a high-tech cloud computing and generative AI innovation campus in Richmond County. This forms part of Amazon's broader $100 billion, 15-year national data center strategy. Similarly, developers have secured zoning for the Kingsboro Data Center Campus in Edgecombe County. Once completed, this 300-acre, 900 MW facility represents a $38 billion capital expenditure and will stand as one of the largest continuous data center campuses in the Southeastern United States.   

3.2 Supply Chain Bottlenecks and Capital Allocation
The velocity of this physical build-out is significantly impeded by global supply chain gridlock for mission-critical electrical and cooling hardware. AI workloads require extreme rack densities, necessitating specialized liquid cooling systems and massive power distribution infrastructure. According to current construction data for the Carolinas, the lead times for these components require hyperscalers to allocate capital and finalize designs years before breaking ground :

These structural delays inherently advantage mega-cap technology companies (Amazon, Microsoft, Google, Meta) which possess the balance sheet liquidity to pre-purchase hardware in massive volumes, potentially crowding out smaller, regional colocation developers from the supply chain entirely.   

4. Energy Policy, Grid Strain, and Economic Incentives
The integration of gigawatt-scale computing facilities into the North Carolina power grid represents the most severe stress test of the state's utility infrastructure in modern history. The relationship between data center proliferation and grid sustainability has become the central regulatory friction point of the decade.   

4.1 The Load Growth Crisis and Ratepayer Affordability
Data centers, which support AI, constitute a small numerical fraction of commercial development projects but account for the overwhelming majority of projected future electricity demand. According to state reporting, data centers account for approximately 80% of Duke Energy's projected load growth. In its 2025 Carolinas Resource Plan, Duke Energy modeled that total net electrical load across its two North Carolina systems will increase between 16% and 60% through 2040. This is a staggering acceleration compared to the preceding two decades (2005–2025), which saw only a 7% total increase in state electricity demand.   

This explosive demand for 24/7 baseload generation—which cannot be reliably met by strictly intermittent renewable sources like solar or wind without massive battery storage—has severe implications for consumer affordability. North Carolina ratepayers have already absorbed a 23% to 30% increase in electricity rates between 2017 and 2024, driven by volatile natural gas pricing, necessary grid modernization, and storm recovery. Major utilities project additional residential rate hikes of 16% to 18% over the next two years.   

Recognizing the acute political and economic risk of everyday consumers subsidizing the power capacity required by multi-trillion-dollar technology firms, Governor Josh Stein issued Executive Order 23 in August 2025, creating the North Carolina Energy Policy Task Force. Co-chaired by Department of Environmental Quality Secretary Reid Wilson and Representative Kyle Hall, the 30-member panel issued a highly critical interim report.   

The Task Force highlighted that data centers possess "uniquely high energy needs" compared to legacy industries such as chemical manufacturing or food processing. To mitigate cost-shifting, the Task Force formulated specific policy recommendations:   

Specialized Rate Structures: Designing new tariff models specifically for hyper-scale loads, ensuring that data centers fully bear the infrastructure costs they impose on the grid.   

Independent Power Procurement: Exploring regulatory pathways that allow massive technology firms to procure and construct their own dedicated power capacity—such as Small Modular Reactors (SMRs) or highly concentrated solar-plus-storage farms—bypassing the residential grid entirely.   

4.2 Aggressive Statutory Tax Incentives
Despite the profound strain on the grid, North Carolina continues to actively solicit data center investments through highly aggressive statutory tax incentives, codified under the North Carolina Competes Act. These incentives drastically reduce the operational expenditures of operating a facility, providing an explicit competitive advantage over neighboring states.   

Exemption Tier	Investment Threshold	Eligibility Requirements	Tax Benefits
Qualifying Data Center	$75 million within 5 years	
Must receive a written determination from the Secretary of Commerce. Must meet county wage standard and health insurance requirements.

The state's aggressive deployment of these incentives has been incredibly successful in attracting capital. However, the Energy Policy Task Force noted a glaring informational deficit: the state government "does not currently have a reliable estimate" of the total dollar value of foregone revenue resulting from these sales tax exemptions. As the infrastructure scales, balancing the macroeconomic benefits of tech sector growth against the localized fiscal and environmental costs will dominate state legislative agendas.   

5. The Artificial Intelligence Ecosystem: Startups and Academic Innovation
While hyperscale data centers provide the physical substrate, North Carolina is simultaneously cultivating the intellectual and corporate capital required to build the algorithmic software layer. The state has fostered a dense symbiosis between Tier-1 research universities and a rapidly expanding ecosystem of venture-backed startups, ensuring that AI development remains a localized economic engine.   

5.1 The Academic Innovation Engine
The Research Triangle—anchored by Duke University, the University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill (UNC), and North Carolina State University (NCSU)—serves as the epicenter of theoretical and applied AI research.   

UNC-Chapel Hill: UNC has systematically integrated AI into its operational and academic frameworks. Initiatives include the deployment of the "BlueChipAI" generative platform tailored for the Kenan-Flagler Business School, and the establishment of an AI Acceleration Program utilizing cloud computing for advanced computational biology and protein design. In FY2025, UNC's commercialization pipeline was remarkably prolific; UNC-affiliated startups generated nearly $8 billion in economic activity, supporting 682 active ventures and contributing $4.29 billion directly to the state's GDP.   

NC State University (NCSU): NCSU dominates in hardware optimization and applied machine learning research. Its highly specialized research centers include the Laboratory for Interpretable Visual Modeling, Computing and Learning (iVMCL) and the Center for Advanced Electronics through Machine Learning (CAEML), the latter formed via a National Science Foundation grant specifically to optimize semiconductor chip design through ML methodologies. Other notable labs include the Optical Sensing Lab (OSL) and Active Robotics Sensing (AROS).   

Duke University: Duke focuses intensely on the intersection of AI, medical ethics, and hardware architecture. The Athena Institute develops advanced edge computing and next-generation communication networks, while the ASIC consortium explores emerging computing platforms for cognitive applications. Furthermore, the CREATE initiative functions as a living lab, deploying AI-based tools directly into educational pedagogies.

6. Tech Hubs, Co-Working, and Regional Ecosystems
The spatial geography of North Carolina's digital economy is actively decentralizing. Driven by a highly educated workforce, affordable cost of living metrics relative to coastal tech hubs, and robust higher education networks, technology clusters are proliferating across multiple distinct economic zones.   

6.1 Regional Tech Hubs Market Map
The Research Triangle (Raleigh, Durham, Chapel Hill): Nationally recognized as the second-fastest growing tech hub in the United States, this region houses over 4,000 technology companies employing 60,000 workers with average salaries exceeding $121,000. A recent national study by CoworkingCafe ranked Durham-Chapel Hill as the #1 mid-size metro area in the nation for STEM careers, citing an unparalleled density of 135 doctorate degrees per 100,000 residents and an average STEM salary of $102,254 (a 14.5% growth rate). Raleigh ranked #3 among all Southern tech hubs (trailing only Austin and Washington D.C.) and placed fourth nationally in doctorate density for large metros.   

Charlotte / Central Piedmont: Historically dominated by finance (the second-largest banking center in the U.S.), Charlotte leverages its massive capital base to incubate financial technology (fintech), cybersecurity, and enterprise software startups. The city ranks 9th among Southern tech metros and boasts a vast footprint of managed IT services and corporate consulting.   

The Piedmont Triad (Greensboro, Winston-Salem, High Point): Transitioning away from its legacy in tobacco, textiles, and furniture manufacturing, the Triad is pivoting toward industrial IoT, logistics tech, and life science research and development.   

Coastal Region (Wilmington) and the Blue Economy: Wilmington has rapidly emerged as a coastal innovation hub. It is characterized by high-growth fintech and health-tech startups, as well as a burgeoning "Blue Economy" focus on ecosystem technologies, aquaculture supply chains, and coastal resilience research.   

Western NC / Mountains (Asheville): Driven by an exceptional quality of life and an influx of remote workers, the mountain region is cultivating early-stage software and spatial computing startups, supported by a growing network of local angel investors and incubators.

7. Market Laws, Regulations, and AI Governance
As physical infrastructure and algorithmic software integrate into critical civil systems, the legal frameworks governing them are undergoing rapid iteration. North Carolina lawmakers are attempting to foster technological supremacy while establishing ethical guardrails—a task vastly complicated by overarching federal policy.

7.1 Telecommunications and Infrastructure Law
The North Carolina Utilities Commission (NCUC) exercises jurisdictional authority over traditional telephony, regulating 16 incumbent local exchange companies (ILECs) and 162 competing local providers (CLPs). However, in strict adherence to modern deregulatory philosophies, the NCUC fundamentally does not regulate data centers, internet service providers, cable television, satellite providers, or commercial mobile radio services. Furthermore, for telecommunications companies operating under N.C.G.S. 62-133.5(h) and (m), the Commission has relinquished control over retail pricing, terms, and conditions, relying entirely on free-market competition to dictate broadband costs.   

To aggressively accelerate the deployment of next-generation wireless networks (particularly 5G and IoT infrastructure), the General Assembly enacted Session Law 2017-159 (House Bill 310). This legislation comprehensively reformed the collocation of small wireless communications infrastructure, severely restricting the ability of local municipalities to delay zoning permits or levy exorbitant fees on telecom providers utilizing public rights-of-way. The law explicitly declared that expeditious infrastructure permitting is a matter of "statewide concern" critical to the global economy.   

Concurrently, the State Chief Information Officer (SCIO) enforces rigorous cyber defense postures via the Statewide Information Security Policies (e.g., Document No. SCIO-SEC-316). This policy mandates strict data classification, encryption protocols, and system protection controls across all state-operated systems and heavily regulates private vendors managing state IT assets.   

7.2 Artificial Intelligence Governance and Legislative Action
The explosion of generative AI has triggered intense legislative scrutiny. However, state-level regulation operates under the immediate threat of federal preemption. In January 2025, the federal government issued Executive Order 14179, "Removing Barriers to American Leadership in Artificial Intelligence". This directive explicitly attempts to preempt states from enacting legislation that creates a fragmented, 50-state regulatory patchwork. It strictly forbids states from passing laws that force AI models to embed ideological biases, mandate specific algorithmic outcomes to avoid "differential treatment," or regulate model outputs beyond state borders.   

In stark contrast to states like Montana (which enacted "Right to Compute" laws protecting critical infrastructure algorithms) or Oregon (which banned AI from utilizing licensed medical professional titles), North Carolina has pivoted away from regulating private-sector foundational models. Instead, the state focuses entirely on internal government governance, procurement standards, and public sector adoption.   

Executive Order No. 24 and the State Governance Framework:
On September 2, 2025, Governor Josh Stein formalized this approach by signing Executive Order No. 24: "Advancing Trustworthy Artificial Intelligence That Benefits All North Carolinians". The directive established a sophisticated, multi-tiered governance apparatus:   

The North Carolina AI Accelerator: Housed within the NCDIT, the Accelerator acts as a centralized government incubator. It mandates that state agencies partner with private businesses and universities in 60-day sprint programs to develop, pilot, and rigorously stress-test AI solutions in secure "sandbox" environments prior to full-scale public deployment.   

Agency AI Oversight Teams: Every state cabinet agency is required to establish an internal oversight team to review proposed AI use cases and conduct thorough risk assessments before software procurement can occur.   

NCDIT Leadership: To manage this complex integration, NCDIT created the role of Deputy Secretary for AI and Policy, appointing I-Sah Hsieh to coordinate all statewide algorithmic initiatives.   

The North Carolina AI Leadership Council:
The cornerstone of EO 24 is the creation of the AI Leadership Council. Co-chaired by NCDIT Secretary Teena Piccione and Department of Commerce Secretary Lee Lilley, the 24-member council is tasked with drafting a comprehensive state AI roadmap, establishing ethical procurement standards, and submitting formalized policy recommendations by June 30, 2026. The council membership deliberately integrates top-tier corporate executives with state regulators, ensuring state policy aligns tightly with enterprise standards:

8. Strategic Outlook and Synergistic Conclusions
North Carolina's rapid ascent as a paramount force in the global digital infrastructure market is the result of highly coordinated economic engineering. The deliberate synergy between aggressive broadband subsidization, hyperscale data center attraction, elite academic research, and pragmatic AI governance has generated an exceptionally robust, future-proofed economic engine. However, the data synthesized throughout this report reveals critical inflection points that will necessitate highly sophisticated, multi-disciplinary policy interventions over the coming decade.

The Grid-Affordability Paradox: The most acute systemic vulnerability in North Carolina's digital strategy resides within its energy grid. The state cannot simultaneously host exponential data center growth (projecting up to 60% localized load increases) and maintain residential rate affordability under traditional, socialized utility models. The swift implementation of the Energy Policy Task Force's recommendations—specifically, the creation of ring-fenced tariffs for hyperscalers and the rapid integration of private, on-site power generation (such as small modular reactors or dedicated utility-scale storage) by technology giants—is absolutely imperative. Failure to decouple industrial technology energy costs from the residential rate base risks severe political backlash and unsustainable inflation for everyday consumers.

Supply Chain as a Structural Barrier to Entry: The extreme lead times for critical data center infrastructure (12-18 months for large transformers, 10-16 months for chillers) inherently consolidates market power among the largest global technology conglomerates. Only hyperscalers possess the immense capital liquidity required to pre-purchase hardware years in advance of breaking ground. This structural bottleneck threatens to stifle the growth of localized, competitive colocation markets, pushing the state further into a monopsony dependency on a handful of mega-cap operators.

The Evolution of the Digital Divide: The unprecedented injection of $1.5 billion in BEAD funding, layered upon aggressive ARPA and RDOF grants, positions North Carolina to theoretically eliminate its rural digital divide by 2030. The strategic reliance on LEO satellites for 30% of the hardest-to-reach Appalachian and coastal topographies represents a fundamental paradigm shift in rural connectivity. Moving forward, the state's strategic focus must inevitably pivot from infrastructure deployment to digital adoption and literacy, ensuring that newly connected populations possess the requisite skills to participate meaningfully in the AI-driven economy.

Pragmatic and Highly Calibrated AI Governance: By explicitly acceding to federal preemption on private-sector algorithmic regulation (via EO 14179) and focusing strictly on internal state-level procurement, public sector sandboxing (through the NCDIT AI Accelerator), and educational integration, North Carolina has successfully avoided the regulatory fragmentation that plagues other tech hubs. This highly pragmatic approach signals a stable, pro-innovation environment to venture capital markets, positioning the state not merely as a passive physical host for server racks, but as a premier architect of the next generation of artificial intelligence.

The successful navigation of these four intersecting vectors—power generation capacity, foundational connectivity, physical supply chain resilience, and precise algorithmic governance—will ultimately determine whether North Carolina sustains its current trajectory as the preeminent digital infrastructure hub of the American Southeast, or buckles under the weight of its own rapid expansion.

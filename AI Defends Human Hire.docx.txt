The AI-Era Evolution of RRK – Navigating the Jagged Frontier
The nature of knowledge work has been fundamentally altered by AI, making a static checklist of skills an obsolete measure of a candidate's true capability. Research from Harvard Business School introduces the pivotal concept of the "jagged technological frontier"—the uneven boundary separating tasks that AI performs well from those it cannot, even when the tasks seem similar in difficulty. The most critical "role-related knowledge" in 2025 and beyond is the ability to accurately identify and navigate this frontier for any given workflow.
The implications are profound: using AI within its frontier can boost a knowledge worker's performance by as much as 43%, while using it outside the frontier actively harms performance, decreasing the quality of work by 19 percentage points. This aligns with the economic framework of distinguishing between routine vs. non-routine tasks. The candidate's expertise lies in their demonstrated ability to automate routine cognitive work (such as data processing, initial code drafting, and report summarization) in order to free up their time and cognitive capacity for high-value, non-routine tasks that require human judgment, creativity, and strategic thinking.
RRK as a Dynamic Portfolio of Human-AI Capabilities
A candidate's true RRK should be viewed not as a list of individual skills, but as a dynamic portfolio management capability. They act as a "manager" of a portfolio of tasks, and their key skill is the strategic allocation of those tasks to the most appropriate "asset"—either their own human cognition or an AI agent—based on the risk-reward profile defined by the jagged frontier.
The traditional view of RRK as a static checklist (e.g., "Knows Python") is no longer sufficient. A skill like "writing code" is now bifurcated: generating boilerplate code and unit tests falls inside the AI frontier, while architecting a novel, complex algorithm lies firmly outside of it. Therefore, simply "knowing Python" is less valuable than knowing when to use an AI code assistant and when to rely on human ingenuity. This transforms RRK from a list of what a person can do to a strategic framework of what a person can orchestrate. Their knowledge resides in the process of production, not just the inputs. This "portfolio management" approach to RRK presents a massive opportunity for Google, as it enables a candidate to produce higher-quality work faster and to scale their expertise across a team by teaching this allocation methodology.

The AI-Era Evolution of Leadership – From Commanding to Orchestrating
The integration of AI into daily workflows is fundamentally transforming the nature of leadership. The traditional model of a leader as a "commander" giving top-down directives is being replaced by the leader as an "orchestrator" of complex systems. This new paradigm requires a leader to curate synergy between human and AI strengths, empower team members to work within this hybrid model, and foster a culture of constant adaptation. The leader's primary role shifts from managing people to designing the environment in which a hybrid human-AI team can thrive.
This evolution demands a new set of leadership skills. Extensive research highlights the necessity of building trust in AI systems, fostering psychological safety for human team members, cultivating cross-disciplinary collaboration, and ensuring rigorous ethical oversight.
Leadership as the Management of Socio-Technical Risk
In the AI era, the most critical leadership challenges are not purely technical or purely human; they are deeply socio-technical. The implementation of AI introduces new and complex risks, including the potential for algorithmic bias, data privacy violations, a lack of transparency, and employee anxiety about job displacement. These risks cannot be mitigated with code alone. They demand a human-centric approach focused on building trust, upholding ethical standards, and shaping a resilient culture.
A leader who focuses only on the technical implementation of AI is destined to fail. True emergent leadership is now demonstrated by the ability to identify and mitigate the risks at the human-AI interface. A leader "emerges" when they proactively address the team's hesitation to trust a new AI tool, recognize the potential for an algorithm to produce biased results, or establish a clear governance framework where one is lacking. Modern emergent leadership is therefore about managing this socio-technical risk. It involves building "psychological safety" so that human team members feel empowered to challenge the AI , ensuring "ethical stewardship" in its deployment , and championing transparency to build trust. This is a far more complex and valuable form of leadership than what was required in the past.

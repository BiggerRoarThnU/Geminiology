The Algorithmic Lexicographer: An Analysis of AI as a Modern Dictionary and Thesaurus and its Transformative Benefits
Introduction - Redefining the Reference Point
From Static Tome to Dynamic Dialogue
The transition from static, authoritative print dictionaries to dynamic, conversational Artificial Intelligence (AI) marks a fundamental paradigm shift in how humanity accesses and interacts with linguistic information. For centuries, the dictionary has served as a stable, curated repository of lexical knowledge, a testament to the painstaking work of human lexicographers who chronicled the evolution of language. This traditional model, even in its digital form, functions primarily as an archive—a reference point to be consulted. The advent of generative AI, however, has transformed this passive resource into an active participant in the act of communication. AI-powered lexical tools function not as static records but as dynamic, conversational partners capable of generating novel linguistic content—definitions, synonyms, examples, and even creative prose—on demand.
This evolution is the culmination of a long-standing trend driven by user preference for speed and convenience, a trend that began with the migration from cumbersome paper volumes to easily searchable digital formats. Yet, the current transformation is more profound than a mere increase in accessibility. It represents a shift from a "dictionary of record," which documents language after its usage has been established, to a "dictionary of possibility," which actively models and influences language use in real time. By generating contextually appropriate sentences and suggesting nuanced vocabulary, AI tools are not just describing the language; they are participating in its ongoing creation. This blurs the traditional lines between descriptive and prescriptive lexicography, creating a feedback loop where the tool's output can shape and potentially accelerate linguistic change. The lexicon is no longer a fixed artifact to be consulted but a living, generative entity to be engaged in dialogue.
Scope and Objectives of the Report
This report provides an exhaustive analysis of this technological and epistemological shift. It aims to deconstruct the underlying technologies that empower AI as a lexicographical tool, offering a rigorous comparative assessment against its traditional counterparts. The analysis will detail the specific benefits and applications for key user demographics, including language learners, professional and creative writers, and the general public.
Furthermore, this report will not shy away from a critical examination of the inherent risks and ethical dilemmas posed by a reliance on these powerful new tools. Issues of accuracy, algorithmic bias, and the potential cognitive consequences of outsourcing linguistic tasks to a machine will be thoroughly investigated. Finally, the report will project the future trajectory of lexicography itself, exploring the evolving role of the human expert in an AI-saturated world and the emergence of a more integrated, "invisible" form of language assistance. By synthesizing academic research, industry analysis, and real-world examples, this document provides a comprehensive and nuanced perspective on the algorithmic lexicographer and its transformative impact on our relationship with language.
The New Lexicon - Under the Hood of AI-Powered Language Tools
Core Technologies: From Rules to Relationships
The ability of AI to function as a sophisticated dictionary and thesaurus is not a single technology but an ecosystem of interconnected concepts. At its core is Natural Language Processing (NLP), the broad field of computer science focused on enabling machines to process, understand, and generate human language. This is achieved through Machine Learning (ML), where systems are not explicitly programmed but learn patterns from vast amounts of data. These models often utilize neural networks, a set of algorithms loosely modeled after the human brain, to recognize complex patterns in text, speech, and images.
The most significant recent advancement is the development of Large Language Models (LLMs), such as those in the GPT (Generative Pre-trained Transformer) and LLAMA series. These models are built on a transformer architecture, a specific type of neural network that excels at tracking relationships and dependencies in sequential data, like the words in a sentence. This architecture allows LLMs to grasp context with unprecedented depth. Instead of just processing words individually, they analyze the entire input to understand how words relate to one another, enabling them to disambiguate meaning, generate coherent text, and provide contextually relevant responses. This ability to understand context is the critical technological leap that separates modern AI lexical tools from their more primitive, rule-based predecessors.
Generative vs. Traditional AI in Lexicography
Within the realm of AI-powered language tools, a crucial distinction exists between traditional and generative AI.
Traditional AI operates on rule-based systems and pattern recognition within structured, labeled datasets. In a lexicographical context, this is the technology behind older spell-checkers, which compare words against a predefined dictionary, or basic digital thesauruses that retrieve synonyms from a manually created list. These systems are effective for specific, well-defined tasks but lack flexibility and cannot create anything new.
Generative AI, in contrast, is defined by its ability to create new and original content. Trained on massive, often unlabeled datasets (such as a significant portion of the internet), these models learn the underlying patterns, grammar, and relationships of language. They can then use this learned knowledge to generate novel text, images, or sounds in response to a prompt. When a user asks a generative AI for the definition of a word in a specific sentence, the AI does not simply look up a pre-written definition. Instead, it generates a new definition tailored to that precise context, along with novel example sentences or even creative content like a mnemonic poem, showcasing a level of dynamic capability far beyond traditional systems.
Algorithmic Approaches to Synonymy
The thesaurus function of AI is a prime example of its sophisticated analytical capabilities. Rather than relying on a human-curated taxonomy like Roget's Thesaurus, AI models determine synonymy through statistical analysis of massive text corpora. This process fundamentally reconceptualizes what a synonym is: not a word belonging to the same logical category, but a word that can be substituted in similar linguistic contexts without fundamentally altering the meaning.
The process typically involves several steps:
Corpus Analysis and Pre-processing: The AI first analyzes user search queries and their resulting actions (e.g., what links they click) to find similar queries that lead to the same results. The text is pre-processed through methods like stemming (reducing words like "connecting" and "connected" to the root "connect") and removing common "stop words" (like "the," "is," "at") to isolate the most meaningful terms.
Contextual Extraction: The algorithm then scans for words and phrases that appear in similar linguistic environments. For example, it might identify "laptop charger" and "laptop power" as potential synonyms because they both frequently appear immediately following the word "laptop" in the training data.
Vector Space Modeling: Modern AI represents words not as text strings but as numerical vectors in a high-dimensional space. Words that are used in similar contexts across billions of sentences will have vectors that are "close" to each other in this space. Synonymy, therefore, becomes a measure of proximity in this abstract semantic space. This allows the AI to identify nuanced and non-obvious relationships that a human might overlook.
Graph Model Refinement: The relationships between these potential synonyms are then often mapped using a graph model. Terms are grouped into clusters based on the likelihood of their relatedness, with stronger relationships appearing closer together on the graph.
This algorithmic approach is powerful but requires careful implementation. For instance, in AI search systems, synonyms are often treated as bidirectional and of equal weight (a search for 'cat' might expand to 'animal', and a search for 'animal' might expand to 'cat'). This necessitates strict rules to prevent logical fallacies, such as treating a subcategory as a synonym (e.g., 'Chromebook' is a type of 'laptop' but not its synonym) or including misspellings that are not valid variants.
A Comparative Assessment - AI versus Traditional Dictionaries
The emergence of AI-powered lexical tools necessitates a rigorous comparison with the traditional dictionaries—both print and basic digital—that have long served as the standard for linguistic reference. This assessment reveals a complex landscape of trade-offs, where AI's dynamic capabilities are balanced against the curated reliability of its predecessors.
User Performance: Receptive vs. Productive Tasks
Academic research provides empirical evidence of how users perform with these different tools. The studies consistently distinguish between two types of linguistic tasks: receptive tasks, which involve understanding or interpreting language (e.g., reading comprehension), and productive tasks, which involve creating language (e.g., writing or speaking).
Productive Tasks: In the realm of language production, AI tools demonstrate a clear and significant advantage. Studies involving English language learners have shown that AI chatbots like ChatGPT are more effective than both monolingual and bilingual dictionaries in helping users accurately produce sentences and use correct collocations (conventional word pairings). The AI's ability to generate contextually appropriate examples and phrasing directly assists the user in the creative act, reducing the cognitive burden of constructing grammatically and idiomatically correct text from scratch.
Receptive Tasks: For language reception, the results are more nuanced. Some research indicates that while AI tools outperform monolingual learner's dictionaries, they may not be superior to high-quality bilingual dictionaries for comprehension tasks. Other studies have found that traditional online dictionaries lead to higher mean scores in receptive tasks, or that there is no statistically significant difference between the tools. This suggests that for the straightforward goal of understanding an unknown word's meaning, the curated, unambiguous, and reliable information presented in a traditional dictionary can be more effective than the potentially variable output of a generative AI.
Core Feature Analysis
A feature-by-feature analysis further illuminates the distinct strengths and weaknesses of each tool type. The primary distinction lies in the reversal of cognitive load. A traditional dictionary requires the user to perform significant cognitive work after receiving the information—they must parse a dense entry, disambiguate between multiple senses, and infer which meaning is appropriate for their context. An AI tool, conversely, shifts this load to the front end; the user must craft an effective and specific prompt to guide the AI, which then performs the task of disambiguation and contextualization. This fundamental difference in interaction design underpins the performance variations across different features.
Speed and Efficiency: AI tools are unequivocally faster. They provide synthesized, direct answers to conversational queries, eliminating the user's need to manually search and filter through lists of results or lengthy entries. The process of consulting a traditional dictionary, whether print or digital, is inherently slower and more deliberate.
Context-Awareness: This is the hallmark advantage of generative AI. An AI can analyze a specific sentence and provide a definition or synonym tailored precisely to that context, a capability that is impossible for a static dictionary. A traditional dictionary lists all possible senses of a word, leaving the user to perform the mental task of selecting the correct one.
Accuracy and Reliability: This remains the primary strength of traditional dictionaries. Compiled and vetted by expert lexicographers, their content is considered highly credible and dependable. AI tools, particularly LLMs, are susceptible to "hallucinations"—the generation of plausible but factually incorrect or nonsensical information. This makes them inherently less reliable for authoritative reference without careful cross-verification by the user.
Interactivity and User Experience: AI tools offer a conversational and interactive user experience that many find more intuitive and engaging than the static search-and-retrieve model of digital dictionaries. The ability to ask follow-up questions and refine queries in natural language creates a more fluid and dynamic interaction.
Depth and Breadth of Information: AI can synthesize information from its vast training data to provide not only definitions and synonyms but also detailed etymologies, a wide array of usage examples, and even novel, creative content such as mnemonic poems upon request. Traditional dictionaries contain a fixed, albeit expertly curated, set of information. However, AI can sometimes struggle with the lexicographical task of "sense splitting," either by omitting valid senses of a word or by unnecessarily subdividing a single sense into multiple definitions.

The AI-Augmented Learner - Revolutionizing Vocabulary Acquisition
The integration of AI into language education is proving to be one of its most impactful applications, fundamentally reshaping the process of vocabulary acquisition. By moving beyond the static, one-size-fits-all model of traditional learning resources, AI introduces a level of personalization, interactivity, and immediate feedback that can significantly accelerate and deepen the learning process.
Personalized and Adaptive Learning Pathways
The core pedagogical advantage of AI is its ability to tailor the learning experience to the unique profile of each student. AI-powered platforms can analyze a learner's performance in real time—tracking their strengths, weaknesses, and learning pace—to create customized educational pathways. If a student consistently struggles with a particular set of words, the system can automatically increase the frequency of exercises related to them. This adaptive approach ensures that learners are always operating within their "Zone of Proximal Development" (ZPD)—the optimal cognitive space where a task is challenging but achievable with guidance.
Traditional methods, such as reading a novel to build vocabulary, are inherently non-adaptive and cannot provide this level of targeted support. In contrast, AI acts as a dynamic scaffold, constantly adjusting the difficulty and focus of the material to match the learner's evolving competence. This personalized instruction has been shown in numerous studies to lead to significant improvements in both vocabulary acquisition and long-term retention.
Enhanced Learning Tools and Gamification
AI transforms passive study tools into dynamic, engaging, and multisensory learning experiences. This "gamification" of the learning process increases student motivation and caters to a wider variety of learning styles.
AI-Powered Flashcards and Quizzes: Applications like Englia allow users to create customizable digital flashcards in seconds. The AI can then use randomized quizzing and spaced repetition algorithms—presenting words at optimal intervals for memorization—to enhance long-term recall far more effectively than manual review.
Rich Contextual Examples: A critical component of deep vocabulary learning is understanding how a word is used in authentic contexts. AI tools excel at this, providing access to a virtually limitless database of real-life sentence examples drawn from their vast training corpora. This allows learners to see a word's nuances, common collocations, and grammatical functions in practice.
Multisensory Engagement: Modern AI tools are increasingly multimodal. They can integrate audio pronunciations to improve listening skills and phonetics, and they can connect words to images and videos to create powerful visual associations. This multisensory approach reinforces learning by engaging multiple cognitive pathways, which is particularly beneficial for visual or auditory learners.
Bridging the Gap to Fluency
For intermediate and advanced learners, AI serves as a powerful "personal language teacher," helping them navigate the more challenging aspects of achieving fluency.
Simplifying Monolingual Dictionaries: A crucial step in language immersion is transitioning from bilingual to monolingual dictionaries, which define words in the target language. This can be a daunting process, as the definitions themselves often contain unfamiliar vocabulary. AI chatbots can act as a bridge, allowing a user to ask for a complex definition to be explained in simpler terms, still within the target language. This provides a graduated path toward engaging with native-level resources.
A Safe Environment for Practice: A significant barrier to language learning is the fear of making mistakes. AI chatbots provide a non-judgmental, consequence-free environment where learners can practice using new words and sentence structures. This freedom to experiment builds confidence and encourages active use of the language.
Immediate, Granular Feedback: Unlike a classroom setting where a teacher's attention is divided, AI tools can provide instantaneous and personalized feedback on a learner's pronunciation, grammar, and vocabulary choices. This immediate correction loop is vital for preventing the fossilization of errors and accelerating the learning cycle.
The Writer's AI-Powered Assistant - Enhancing Professional and Creative Expression
For professional writers, content creators, and anyone for whom clear and effective communication is paramount, AI-powered lexical tools have become indispensable assistants. They move far beyond the capabilities of a simple spell-checker or traditional thesaurus, offering sophisticated support that enhances creativity, improves quality, and streamlines the entire writing workflow.
Beyond Synonymy: The Power of Contextual Word Choice
The most profound benefit for writers is the AI's ability to provide context-aware synonym suggestions. A traditional thesaurus presents a static list of alternatives, leaving the writer to determine which word best fits the specific nuance, tone, and semantic context of their sentence. This process is fraught with peril, as selecting an ill-fitting synonym is a hallmark of amateur writing.
Generative AI tools overcome this limitation by analyzing the entire sentence or even the surrounding paragraph. When a writer highlights a word or phrase, the AI doesn't just look for words with similar dictionary definitions; it searches for alternatives that are statistically likely to appear in a similar linguistic context. This allows it to suggest words that are not only semantically similar but also stylistically and tonally appropriate. Furthermore, advanced tools can suggest replacements for entire phrases, transforming a mundane expression like "pondered sadly" into a more evocative one like "weighed with a heavy heart," thereby elevating the quality of the prose in a way a word-for-word thesaurus cannot. This transforms the tool from a passive reference into an active, dialogic partner in the creative process, allowing the writer to iterate and refine their expression in collaboration with the machine.
Streamlining the Writing Workflow
AI serves as a powerful force multiplier, automating and assisting with many of the most time-consuming aspects of writing, which allows creators to focus on higher-level strategic and creative tasks.
Accelerating Research and Drafting: AI tools can act as powerful research assistants, quickly summarizing articles and extracting key facts, which significantly reduces the time spent on preliminary research. They can also help overcome the "blank page" problem by generating initial drafts, outlines, or a variety of headlines based on a simple prompt, providing a solid starting point for the writer to then refine and build upon.
Improving Readability and Style: Beyond simple grammar correction, AI assistants can function as sophisticated style editors. They can identify and suggest improvements for convoluted sentences, detect overuse of the passive voice, help adjust the tone of a piece to be more formal or casual, and flag repetitive words and phrases. This helps writers produce clearer, more concise, and more engaging content.
Specialized Applications
The versatility of AI allows for its application in a range of specialized writing contexts.
Search Engine Optimization (SEO): AI tools are adept at generating SEO-optimized content. They can produce multiple variations of headlines and meta descriptions designed to attract clicks and can help writers seamlessly integrate relevant keywords into their articles, improving their visibility on search engines.
Support for Non-Native Writers: For individuals writing in a language that is not their first, AI assistants are an invaluable resource. They provide real-time feedback on grammatical errors, awkward phrasing, and incorrect idioms, helping users produce text that is not only correct but also sounds natural and fluent.
Technical and Professional Communication: Professionals who are not trained writers, such as scientists, engineers, or financial analysts, can use AI to transform technical data and jargon-filled notes into clear, readable, and well-structured reports or presentations. This democratizes the ability to produce high-quality written communication across all fields.

The Cognitive Paradox and Ethical Minefield
Despite their transformative potential, the widespread adoption of AI-powered language tools is accompanied by significant risks and profound ethical questions. The very efficiency that makes these tools so appealing may come at a cognitive cost, creating a paradox where technological assistance could lead to the erosion of fundamental human intellectual skills. This section critically examines the limitations of AI accuracy, the problem of algorithmic bias, and the emerging evidence of the technology's impact on human cognition.
The Accuracy Problem: Hallucinations and Misinformation
A primary and well-documented limitation of current LLMs is their propensity to "hallucinate"—that is, to generate responses that are fluent, confident, and plausible-sounding but are factually incorrect, nonsensical, or entirely fabricated. Because these models are probabilistic systems designed to predict the next likely word in a sequence, they do not possess a true understanding of facts or truth. This makes an uncritical reliance on AI for definitive information inherently risky. While AI-generated definitions can be comparable to those written by human lexicographers, studies have shown that the accompanying example sentences and overall dictionary entries are often rated as lower quality by experts. Consequently, any information retrieved from an AI, particularly factual claims or precise definitions, must be cross-verified with authoritative sources to ensure accuracy.
Algorithmic Bias: Reflecting and Amplifying Societal Prejudices
AI models learn from the data they are trained on, which consists of vast swathes of text and images from the internet. This data is a reflection of human society, complete with its inherent biases, stereotypes, and inequalities. As a result, AI models can inadvertently absorb, replicate, and even amplify these biases in their outputs. This can manifest in numerous ways within a linguistic context:
Gender Bias: Associating professions with specific genders (e.g., "doctor" with male pronouns, "nurse" with female pronouns).
Racial and Cultural Bias: Perpetuating stereotypes or generating content that is culturally insensitive or offensive.
Linguistic Bias: Favoring dominant languages, dialects, and accents while performing less accurately for or marginalizing speakers of less common variations.
This issue is a critical ethical concern, as biased AI tools can reinforce harmful societal norms and create inequitable experiences for users from underrepresented groups. Addressing this requires a concerted effort in curating more diverse and representative training data and designing algorithms with fairness as a core principle. This has also led to a re-conceptualization of the modern lexicographer's role, which is shifting to include the crucial task of identifying and counteracting these biases in AI systems.
The Cognitive Impact: Efficiency at the Cost of Intellect?
Perhaps the most significant long-term concern is the impact of AI on human cognitive development. The seamless efficiency of AI tools may encourage a form of intellectual outsourcing that weakens our own mental faculties.
Cognitive Offloading: Research has identified a phenomenon known as "cognitive offloading," where individuals delegate cognitive tasks such as memory, analysis, and problem-solving to external tools. While humans have always used tools to offload cognition (from writing to calculators), the ease and ubiquity of AI may be accelerating this process to a detrimental degree.
Erosion of Critical Thinking: Studies have found a significant negative correlation between frequent AI tool usage and critical thinking abilities. When an AI provides an instant, well-formed answer, it reduces the user's need to engage in the difficult but essential processes of analyzing information, evaluating evidence, and synthesizing a conclusion independently.
Memory and Learning Impairment: A groundbreaking study from MIT used electroencephalography (EEG) to measure the brain activity of students writing essays. The results were stark: participants using ChatGPT exhibited the weakest brain connectivity and lowest engagement. They struggled to recall or accurately quote from the essays they had just written, suggesting that the use of the AI assistant bypassed the deep cognitive processes necessary for encoding information into long-term memory. This raises serious concerns that over-reliance on AI could actively harm the learning process.
Homogenization of Creativity: The same MIT study noted that the essays produced with AI assistance were "soulless" and remarkably similar to one another, lacking original thought and relying on formulaic expressions. This suggests that instead of augmenting human creativity, AI might inadvertently stifle it by promoting a reliance on common, statistically probable patterns, leading to more homogenous and less innovative outputs.
This combination of high efficiency and cognitive offloading can create a perilous feedback loop, an "illusion of competence." A user can produce a high-quality output (e.g., a well-written email) in a fraction of the time, leading to a feeling of enhanced productivity and skill. However, because the AI performed the heavy cognitive lifting—structuring the argument, choosing the right words, correcting grammar—the user has not actually practiced or strengthened their own abilities. Over time, this can lead to an increased dependency on the tool as the user's unassisted skills may atrophy, even as their confidence in their AI-mediated performance grows. This cognitive paradox lies at the heart of the debate over the long-term societal impact of these technologies.
Educational and Societal Risks
These cognitive and ethical issues translate into tangible risks for education and society at large. In academia, the ease with which AI can generate text poses an unprecedented challenge to academic integrity, making plagiarism and cheating easier than ever. More fundamentally, there is a risk of widespread deskilling, where a generation of students may fail to develop the foundational critical thinking and writing skills necessary for academic and professional success because they have become overly dependent on technology to perform these tasks for them.
The Future of Lexicography and Language Interaction
The disruptive force of artificial intelligence is not signaling the end of lexicography but is instead catalyzing its most significant transformation in centuries. The future of the field will be defined by a new relationship between human expertise and machine intelligence, leading to a redefinition of the lexicographer's role and the very nature of a "dictionary." Language assistance is poised to become more deeply integrated, personalized, and "invisible," embedding itself into the fabric of our digital interactions.
The Evolving Role of the Human Lexicographer
The lexicographer of the future will transition from being a primary author of dictionary content to a high-level architect, curator, and ethicist of AI language systems. As AI automates the more routine tasks of data collection, definition drafting, and synonym identification, the unique value of human experts will shift to more complex, judgment-based functions that machines currently cannot perform.
Key responsibilities in this new role will include:
AI Training and Guidance: Designing prompts, creating "gold standard" datasets for training and testing models, and fine-tuning AI systems to improve the quality and accuracy of their lexicographical output.
Quality Assurance and Post-Editing: Acting as expert reviewers who validate, correct, and refine AI-generated content, much like a translator post-edits a machine translation.
Ethical Oversight and Bias Mitigation: A crucial function will be to identify and counteract the algorithmic biases present in LLMs. This involves ensuring that language models represent language equitably, avoid perpetuating harmful stereotypes, and are culturally sensitive.
Preserving Linguistic Diversity: While LLMs excel with data-rich languages like English, they perform poorly on less-documented or endangered languages. Human lexicographers will be essential for fieldwork, data collection, and working in these "data-sparse environments" to ensure these languages are represented in our digital future and to preserve global linguistic heritage.
This evolution demands a new, interdisciplinary skillset that merges traditional linguistic expertise with principles of data science, computational linguistics, and AI ethics. The future of lexicography lies not in defining individual words, but in curating the meaning, nuance, and ethics of the AI's entire linguistic world model.
The Rise of "Invisible" and Integrated Lexicography
The concept of the dictionary as a discrete, standalone product—a book to be opened or a website to be visited—is rapidly becoming obsolete. The future lies in what has been termed "invisible lexicography," where lexical data and AI-powered language assistance are seamlessly integrated into the digital tools we use every day.
Early examples of this trend are already prevalent. Browser extensions like WordSensei provide instant, AI-powered definitions and synonyms for any word selected on a webpage, eliminating the need to open a new tab. Smart keyboards like the Thesaurus Synonyms AI Keyboard integrate contextual synonym suggestions directly into the typing interface of any application, from messaging apps to email clients. This trend will only accelerate, with language support becoming a background utility that is deeply embedded in operating systems, writing software, and communication platforms, providing assistance that is proactive and contextually aware.
Future Trajectories and Recommendations
To harness the immense benefits of AI in language while mitigating its significant risks, a concerted and strategic approach is required from educators, developers, and the lexicographical community.
For Education: Educational institutions must shift from prohibiting AI use to teaching critical engagement with it. Curricula should include instruction on prompt engineering (the skill of crafting effective queries), digital literacy to identify and evaluate AI-generated content, and a renewed emphasis on the writing process—drafting, peer review, and revision—over the final, polished product. It is imperative to balance the integration of AI tools with pedagogical strategies that continue to foster unassisted critical thinking, memory, and creativity.
For Developers: The goal should be to design AI tools that function as cognitive scaffolds rather than cognitive replacements. This involves building in features that promote user agency and deep learning. For example, systems could be designed with greater transparency, explaining why a particular grammatical or stylistic suggestion is being made, thereby teaching the underlying principle. Developers also bear a significant ethical responsibility to actively address and mitigate bias in their training data and algorithms to ensure their products are fair and inclusive.
For Lexicography: The field must fully embrace its transformation. This requires fostering deep, interdisciplinary collaboration between lexicographers and computer scientists to build the next generation of intelligent and ethical language tools. A primary mission for the field should be to champion and work towards the preservation of linguistic diversity, focusing resources on the less-documented languages that are currently being left behind by the data-hungry nature of AI development. By doing so, lexicography can ensure that the future of language is as rich and diverse as its past.
